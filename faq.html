Frequently Asked Questions

Evidence-based answers about BMI, metabolic health, and how to use these calculators effectively.

+ Is BMI accurate for everyone?

No. BMI is a population-level screening tool, not an individual diagnostic. It does not account for:

  • Muscle mass: Athletes and bodybuilders can have "obese" BMIs with low body fat
  • Bone density: Individuals with higher bone mass may have elevated BMI
  • Fat distribution: Visceral fat (around organs) is more dangerous than subcutaneous fat, but BMI treats them equally
  • Ethnicity: Asian populations develop metabolic complications at lower BMI values
  • Age: BMI thresholds should arguably be adjusted for older adults

Always use BMI as a starting point, not an endpoint. Combine with waist circumference, body composition assessment, and metabolic markers for a complete picture.

+ Why do Asian populations need different BMI thresholds?

Asian populations develop metabolic complications — type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, hypertension — at lower BMI values than Caucasian populations. The reasons involve genetic factors, body fat distribution patterns, and possibly epigenetic influences.

WHO Western Pacific / IDF Guidelines for Asians:

  • Overweight: BMI ≥ 23 (not 25)
  • Obese: BMI ≥ 25 (not 30)
  • Central obesity (men): Waist ≥ 90 cm
  • Central obesity (women): Waist ≥ 80 cm

South Asian populations (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi) may have even lower thresholds. If you're Asian and your doctor dismisses a BMI of 24 as "normal," show them the WHO Western Pacific guidelines.

+ Can I use adult BMI calculators for my child?

No. Children require age and sex-specific BMI percentiles, not adult BMI categories. A BMI of 20 means something completely different for a 5-year-old than a 15-year-old.

Pediatric BMI Categories (CDC Growth Charts):

  • Underweight: < 5th percentile
  • Healthy weight: 5th-84th percentile
  • Overweight: 85th-94th percentile
  • Obese: ≥ 95th percentile

What matters most is the growth trajectory — how a child's percentile changes over time — not a single measurement. A child tracking at the 90th percentile since age 2 may be genetically predisposed to a larger frame. A child jumping from the 50th to the 90th percentile warrants investigation.

Always consult a pediatrician for comprehensive assessment. Never put a child on a restrictive diet without medical supervision.

+ What is waist-to-height ratio and why does it matter?

Waist-to-height ratio (WHtR) is your waist circumference divided by your height. It's one of the simplest and most predictive measures of metabolic risk.

Thresholds:

  • < 0.5: Low metabolic risk
  • 0.5-0.6: Moderate risk
  • > 0.6: High risk

WHtR captures visceral fat — the dangerous fat wrapped around your organs that drives insulin resistance, inflammation, and cardiovascular disease. BMI completely misses this. I've had patients with "normal" BMIs but 40-inch waists whose metabolic panels looked like disaster zones.

Measure at the midpoint between your lowest rib and the top of your hip bone (iliac crest), after a normal breath out. Don't suck in.

+ Why does BMI classify muscular people as overweight or obese?

Because BMI only considers total weight relative to height. It cannot distinguish between muscle, bone, fat, and water. Muscle is about 15% denser than fat per unit volume, so muscular individuals weigh more without being overfat.

Examples from my practice:

  • NFL lineman: BMI 34, body fat 14% — metabolically healthy
  • Powerlifter: BMI 31, body fat 12% — competes nationally
  • CrossFit athlete: BMI 27, body fat 16% — runs 6-minute miles

If you're athletic, track body fat percentage (DEXA or Bod Pod) and performance metrics instead of BMI. BMI is meaningless for athletes.

+ How often should I measure my BMI or waist circumference?

For most adults, monthly measurements are sufficient. Daily weigh-ins create data noise — weight fluctuates with hydration, sodium intake, menstrual cycle, and bowel habits. Weekly measurements are okay if you're actively trying to lose weight. Daily is overkill and often counterproductive.

Best practices:

  • Measure at the same time of day (morning, after bathroom, before eating)
  • Use the same scale and tape measure
  • Wear similar clothing (or none)
  • Track trends over 3+ months, not daily fluctuations
  • Focus on waist circumference — it's more stable and more predictive than weight

The BMI Trend Tracker on this site is designed for monthly entries. Use it to see your 12-week moving average, not daily noise.

+ What should I do if my BMI says I'm overweight but I feel healthy?

Trust your body — but verify with data. "Feeling healthy" and being metabolically healthy aren't always the same thing. I've had patients who felt fine until their fasting glucose hit 130.

Get checked:

  • Fasting glucose and HbA1c (diabetes screening)
  • Lipid panel (cholesterol, triglycerides, HDL)
  • Blood pressure
  • Waist circumference
  • Thyroid function (TSH) if you have fatigue or cold intolerance

If all of these are normal and you're physically active, your "overweight" BMI may simply reflect muscle mass or a larger frame. BMI is a screening tool. It's not a verdict.
