Frequently Asked Questions

Evidence-based answers to common BMI questions from a practicing physician.

What is BMI and how is it calculated?

BMI (Body Mass Index) is calculated by dividing your weight in kilograms by your height in meters squared. The formula is: BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)².

For example, if you weigh 70kg and are 1.75m tall, your BMI would be 70 / (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9.

You can also use pounds and inches: BMI = weight(lbs) × 703 / height(in)².

Is BMI accurate for everyone?

No, BMI is not accurate for everyone. It's a useful screening tool but has well-documented limitations:

• Athletes and bodybuilders: High muscle mass can push BMI into "overweight" or "obese" categories despite low body fat.

• Older adults: BMI may underestimate health risk because it doesn't account for age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia).

• Pregnant women: Weight gain is expected and healthy during pregnancy.

• Children and teens: BMI is interpreted differently using age and sex-specific percentiles.

For a more complete picture, consider waist circumference, body fat percentage, and other health markers alongside BMI.

What is a healthy BMI range?

According to WHO standards, a healthy BMI is 18.5-24.9.

However, Asian populations have lower thresholds due to higher metabolic risk at lower BMI levels:

• Normal: 18.5-22.9
• Overweight: 23-24.9
• Obese: 25+

These ethnicity-specific thresholds are supported by the World Health Organization Western Pacific Region and multiple Asian health organizations.

Why does my BMI say I'm overweight when I look fine?

This is one of the most common questions I get in my clinic. Here's the honest answer:

BMI measures mass, not health. You could have a BMI of 26 ("overweight") but have 15% body fat and excellent cardiovascular fitness. Conversely, someone with a "normal" BMI of 22 could have high visceral fat and poor metabolic health.

What matters more than BMI:

• Waist circumference (men >40in, women >35in is concerning)
• Body fat percentage
• Blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar
• Fitness level

If your BMI says overweight but you feel great and your doctor says your labs are good, BMI is just one data point. Don't let a number define you.

Can BMI predict health problems?

BMI is correlated with health risks, but it's not a perfect predictor.

Research shows that higher BMI is associated with increased risk of:

• Type 2 diabetes
• Cardiovascular disease
• Certain cancers
• Sleep apnea
• Joint problems

However, BMI alone doesn't tell the full story. Two people with the same BMI can have very different health profiles based on their body composition, lifestyle, and genetics.

Bottom line: Use BMI as a starting point for conversation with your doctor, not as a final verdict on your health.

How often should I check my BMI?

Honestly? Not as often as you think.

Unless you're actively trying to gain or lose weight, checking BMI once every 3-6 months is plenty. Daily or weekly weighing creates unnecessary stress and doesn't give you meaningful data.

What I tell my patients: track trends, not daily numbers. Your weight fluctuates 2-3 pounds daily due to water, salt, hormones, and digestion. That's normal biology, not failure.

If you're on a weight management plan, monthly BMI checks are reasonable. Otherwise, relax about the number and focus on how you feel, your energy levels, and your lab results.

What's the difference between BMI and body fat percentage?

BMI uses only height and weight. It can't tell you how much of your weight is muscle vs. fat.

Body fat percentage measures the actual proportion of fat in your body. This gives a much more accurate picture of body composition.

Healthy body fat ranges:

• Men: 10-20%
• Women: 20-30%

Athletes often have lower percentages (men 6-13%, women 14-20%), while essential fat minimums are about 2-5% for men and 10-13% for women.
