BMI Chart 2026: Standard Weight Ranges by Height

Published May 7, 2026 · 6 min read

When I worked at the nutrition clinic in 2023, the most common question was not "how do I calculate BMI?" It was "what should I actually weigh?" People do not want formulas. They want a chart they can look at, point to, and say "there, that is my range."

This page gives exactly that. Two charts — one metric, one imperial. Color-coded because staring at raw numbers is boring and confusing.

How to Read These Charts

Find your height in the left column. Look across to see your weight range. The colors mean:

  • Yellow = Underweight (BMI < 18.5)
  • Green = Normal (BMI 18.5-24.9)
  • Orange = Overweight (BMI 25-29.9)
  • Red = Obese (BMI 30+)

What These Numbers Do Not Tell You

I saw patients obsess over these charts at the clinic. Like, genuinely stressed because they were 0.5 kg into the orange zone. Here is what the chart cannot see:

Muscle vs. fat. A muscular person may fall in "overweight" while being very healthy. The chart treats a bodybuilder and a couch potato the same if they have the same height and weight. Which is ridiculous, but that is how BMI works.

Fat distribution. Belly fat is way more dangerous than hip fat. BMI cannot tell where your fat lives. Two people with identical BMI can have completely different health risks based on waist size.

Age matters. Older adults may benefit from slightly higher BMI (22-27) for bone health. My grandmother at BMI 21 was frail. At BMI 24, she was stronger and healthier. Her doctor actually wanted her to gain weight.

Ethnicity. Asian populations often have higher health risks at lower BMI thresholds. The standard WHO chart is based largely on European populations. If you are Asian, your "normal" might actually be lower.
