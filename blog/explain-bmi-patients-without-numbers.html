How I Explain BMI to Patients Who Don't Care About Numbers

D. Chen 2026-05-03 6 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
"Doctor, just tell me if I'm healthy. I don't want a lecture about numbers." I hear this maybe... once a day? From patients who are sick of BMI, sick of scales, sick of being reduced to a digit. And honestly? I get it. I've been that doctor who rattles off BMI, body fat %, and metabolic markers while the patient's eyes glaze over. So I changed how I talk about it. Here are the frameworks I use when numbers don't land: **The clothing analogy:** "BMI is like a clothing size. A size Large tells you something about the garment, but it doesn't tell you if it's a thick winter coat or a thin t-shirt. It doesn't tell you the fabric quality. It just gives you a rough category. Your body is the same — BMI gives a rough category, but what's inside matters more." **The car analogy:** "Your weight is like the total weight of a car. A heavy car could be a reinforced truck built for hauling, or it could be a rusted-out sedan with a broken engine. Same weight, completely different condition. We need to look under the hood — your blood work, your blood pressure, how you feel day to day." **The weather analogy:** "BMI is like the average temperature for a city. It tells you something about the climate, but it doesn't tell you if it's raining today. Your metabolic markers — glucose, cholesterol, blood pressure — are the daily weather. That's what we treat." **The bank account analogy (for financial types):** "Your BMI is like your total net worth. It gives a snapshot, but it doesn't tell us about cash flow, debt structure, or investment quality. Two people with the same net worth can have completely different financial health. Same with BMI and metabolic health."

**What I actually measure:** When patients say they don't care about BMI, I pivot to what I call the "Big Five" — the things that actually predict health outcomes: 1. **Blood pressure** — silent killer, no symptoms until damage is done 2. **Fasting glucose / A1C** — diabetes develops quietly for years 3. **Lipid panel** — heart disease risk, invisible until it's not 4. **Waist circumference** — where fat lives matters more than how much 5. **How you feel** — energy, sleep, mood, digestion. Subjective but real. I had a patient last year, BMI 31, who came in convinced she was "fat and unhealthy." Her BP was 118/72. Glucose 89. LDL 112. She slept 8 hours, hiked on weekends, felt great. Was she at elevated risk compared to BMI 22? Probably. Was she "unhealthy"? Not by any meaningful clinical definition. Another patient, BMI 22, picture of "normal." BP 148/92. Glucose 118. Felt exhausted all the time but assumed it was stress. She was metabolically sick and didn't know it because her BMI said she was fine. **The honest conversation:** "Look, I can give you numbers all day. But here's what matters: are your organs working well? Is your heart not straining? Is your pancreas keeping up? Is your liver not turning into fat? BMI doesn't answer those questions. Blood work does. How you feel does. Let's check those instead." Most patients respond to this. It shifts the conversation from weight (emotional, stigmatized, often shame-inducing) to function (practical, medical, actionable). And sometimes, after we check the Big Five and they're all good, I tell the patient: "Your BMI is elevated, but your body is functioning well. We should monitor trends, but I'm not worried about you today." The relief on their face is worth more than any weight loss lecture. D. Chen P.S. If you're a patient reading this, print it out and bring it to your next appointment. If your doctor won't look past BMI, this might start a useful conversation. Or it might help you find a doctor who already thinks this way.
