What 15 Years of Metabolic Data Actually Looks Like

David Chen 2026-05-12 8 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
I've been tracking metabolic markers in my practice since around 2011. Not systematically at first — just notes in patient files, trends I noticed, patterns that kept showing up. Around 2016 I started actually logging things in a spreadsheet. Don't tell my IT department. The data is messy. That's the first thing. Real clinical data is always messy. Patients miss appointments, labs get drawn at different times of day, people change insurance and disappear for two years then show up again with completely different numbers. But patterns emerge anyway. Here are three that surprised me: **Pattern 1: The BMI 27 plateau** I have maybe... 40 patients? No, probably closer to 60 who have been stuck at BMI 27-28 for years. Not gaining, not losing. Their diets vary wildly — some eat clean, some don't. Their exercise ranges from sedentary to gym rats. But their weight doesn't budge. Their metabolic panels? Also remarkably stable. Fasting glucose 95-102. A1C 5.4-5.7. Blood pressure 125/78-ish. Are they unhealthy? By BMI standards, yes — overweight. By metabolic standards? Mostly fine. A few have developed pre-diabetes, but most haven't. I've started wondering if BMI 27 is just a stable setpoint for some people, metabolically speaking. The literature on setpoint theory is controversial, but my patient data makes me think there's something to it. **Pattern 2: The rapid BMI 30+ group** This one worries me. Patients who go from BMI 24 to 32 in under two years. It's not gradual — it's a cliff. Usually triggered by a life event: divorce, job loss, new medication (steroids, antipsychotics), or sometimes nothing obvious at all. Their metabolic markers crash fast. Fasting glucose goes from 90 to 115 in six months. Triglycerides double. The interesting part? They respond to intervention better than the chronic overweight group. Lose 5% body weight and their glucose normalizes. It's like their metabolism is still "remembering" how to be healthy. The chronic group? Much harder. Their bodies seem to have adapted to the higher weight metabolically.

**Pattern 3: The "healthy obese" paradox** I have... maybe 8 patients? With BMI >30 but completely normal metabolic profiles. Glucose fine, lipids fine, blood pressure fine, no fatty liver. They exercise regularly, eat reasonably. One is a powerlifter, BMI 31, body fat probably 18%. Two are postmenopausal women who gained weight but maintained metabolic health. How long does this last? I'm not sure. The longest I've tracked is about 7 years. Will they eventually develop problems? Probably. But the "obese = unhealthy" narrative is too simple. Metabolic health matters more than BMI category, at least in the short to medium term. **What this means for you:** Don't trust a single BMI reading. Track trends. If your BMI has been stable for years and your metabolic markers are good, you're probably fine. If you're climbing rapidly, that's the danger zone — regardless of where you started. And find a doctor who looks at more than BMI. Ask for fasting glucose, A1C, lipid panel, blood pressure, and liver enzymes at minimum. Waist circumference too — it's a better predictor of metabolic risk than BMI alone. The data isn't perfect. My sample is biased — mostly insured, mostly urban, mostly compliant patients who actually show up. But it's real-world data, not a clinical trial with exclusion criteria. And it tells a more nuanced story than "BMI good = healthy, BMI bad = sick." Dr. David Chen P.S. I'm thinking about publishing this data properly, anonymized of course. Need to find a biostatistician who can handle the messiness. If you know one, send them my way.
