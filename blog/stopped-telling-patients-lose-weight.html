I Stopped Telling Patients to 'Lose Weight' — Here's What I Say Instead

— Chen 2026-05-08 7 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
For the first 8 years of my practice, I was that doctor. "You need to lose 20 pounds." "Your BMI is too high." "Have you tried diet and exercise?" It didn't work. Not because my patients were non-compliant. Because I was giving them a destination without a map, and shaming them in the process. The research on weight stigma is pretty clear now. Patients who feel stigmatized by their doctors are less likely to seek care, more likely to avoid preventive services, and — ironically — more likely to engage in disordered eating. A 2015 study in Patient Education and Counseling found that weight stigma in clinical settings predicted weight gain, not loss, over 4 years. So around 2019, I changed my approach. I stopped talking about weight loss as the goal. Now I talk about metabolic health. And the results are... different. Not always better in terms of pounds lost, but better in terms of actual health outcomes. **What I say now:** Instead of "lose weight," I say "improve your metabolic markers." Specific, measurable, less emotionally loaded. We pick 2-3 targets: get fasting glucose below 100. Get blood pressure below 130/80. Get LDL below 100. These are concrete goals with concrete strategies. Instead of "eat less," I ask "what's one meal you could change this week?" Small, achievable. One patient started by switching from sugary coffee drinks to black coffee. Lost 8 pounds in 3 months without trying to lose weight. Another added a 10-minute walk after dinner. A1C dropped from 6.1 to 5.6. Instead of "exercise more," I ask "what movement do you enjoy?" One woman discovered she loved swimming at the local YMCA. A guy found out he could bike to work in 20 minutes. If they hate the gym, I don't send them to the gym. **The 5% rule:**

I talk a lot about 5% body weight loss. Not 20%, not 50%. Five percent. The research shows that 5% loss improves glucose, lipids, blood pressure, and liver enzymes significantly. For a 200-pound person, that's 10 pounds. Achievable in 3-6 months with modest changes. And it doesn't require crash dieting or extreme exercise. Most of my patients can lose 5%. Many can lose 10%. Very few can lose 30% and keep it off for 5 years. The data on long-term weight loss maintenance is... sobering. So I set them up for success, not failure. **When weight IS the problem:** I'm not anti-weight-loss. For patients with BMI >35 and metabolic disease, significant weight loss can be transformative. I've referred patients for bariatric surgery when appropriate. I've prescribed GLP-1 agonists for diabetics with obesity. These are medical interventions for medical problems. But for the patient with BMI 29, glucose 102, blood pressure 128/82? The "lose weight" conversation often does more harm than good. Focus on the metabolic markers. The weight often follows, but even if it doesn't, the health improves. **The honest truth:** Some of my patients lose weight and keep it off. Some don't. Some lose it and regain it. Some never lose it but their metabolic health improves anyway through medication, movement, and dietary changes that don't show up on the scale. I used to think my job was to make people thinner. Now I think my job is to make people healthier, however that looks for them. Sometimes it looks like weight loss. Sometimes it doesn't. Both are valid outcomes. — Chen P.S. If your doctor's only metric for health is your weight, find a new doctor. Or show them this article. Maybe it'll help.
