BMI vs. Body Fat Percentage: Which Actually Predicts Health?

Dr. David Chen 2026-05-05 6 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
I get this question at least twice a week. "Doctor, my scale says I'm 28% body fat. But my BMI is normal. Which should I believe?" My answer: believe both, but understand what each measures. And understand that neither tells the whole story. **What BMI actually measures:** BMI is mass divided by height squared. That's it. It doesn't know if that mass is muscle, fat, bone, or water. A bodybuilder with 8% body fat can have BMI 30+. A frail elderly person with 35% body fat can have BMI 20. Same number, completely different health profiles. **What body fat percentage measures:** Body fat % tells you the proportion of fat mass to total mass. In theory, this should be more useful. And for many people, it is. The American College of Sports Medicine defines healthy ranges as 10-22% for men and 20-32% for women. Above those ranges, metabolic risk increases. But here's the problem: measuring body fat accurately is hard. **The measurement problem:** Bioelectrical impedance scales — the ones most people have at home — are notoriously variable. Hydration status, time of day, recent exercise, even foot calluses affect the reading. I've seen the same person measure 22% at 6 AM and 27% at 8 PM. That's not real body fat change. That's measurement error. DEXA scans are accurate but expensive and not widely available. Bod Pod is good but requires specialized equipment. Skinfold calipers depend heavily on technician skill. Underwater weighing is the gold standard but impractical for routine use. So when a patient tells me their body fat percentage, my first question is: how was it measured? Even accurate body fat % has a blind spot: where the fat is located. Two people can both be 25% body fat. One carries it subcutaneously — under the skin, mostly hips and thighs. The other carries it viscerally — around the organs, in the abdomen. The second person has much higher metabolic risk, but body fat % alone won't tell you that. This is why waist circumference matters. Multiple studies show that waist-to-height ratio predicts metabolic risk better than either BMI or body fat % alone. A ratio >0.5 (waist more than half your height) indicates elevated risk, regardless of BMI. **What I use in practice:** I look at all three: BMI, body fat % (if measured reliably), and waist circumference. Plus metabolic markers: glucose, lipids, blood pressure, liver enzymes. For most patients, BMI is a fine starting point. It's easy to calculate, everyone understands it, and population-level data is extensive. But if BMI is borderline, or if the patient is very muscular, or if metabolic markers don't match the BMI category, I dig deeper. Body fat % is useful when measured well. If a patient has access to DEXA or reliable caliper measurements, I incorporate it. But I don't trust home scale readings for clinical decisions. Waist circumference is the most underutilized tool. It's cheap, fast, and predictive. Every physical exam should include it. I measure it on every patient, every visit. **The bottom line:** BMI is a population screening tool that works reasonably well for average people. Body fat % is more specific but harder to measure accurately. Neither replaces metabolic testing. And neither tells you where the fat is, which matters enormously. Use BMI as a quick check. Use body fat % if you have reliable measurements. Always measure your waist. And get your metabolic markers checked regularly. Health is multidimensional. Any single number that claims to capture it is lying to you. Dr. David Chen P.S.
