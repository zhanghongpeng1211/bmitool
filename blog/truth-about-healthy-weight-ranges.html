The Truth About 'Healthy Weight' Ranges Nobody Talks About

David Chen 2026-04-28 6 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
"Your healthy weight is 58 to 78 kg." I saw this on a popular BMI calculator last week. For a 5'6" woman, that's a 20-kilogram range. Forty-four pounds. From clinically underweight to borderline overweight. And the tool presented it with the same confidence it used for the BMI calculation itself. This is absurd. And it's everywhere. **Where 'healthy weight' ranges come from:** They're simply the weight equivalents of BMI 18.5 to 24.9 for a given height. That's it. There's no additional science. No consideration of age, sex, body composition, ethnicity, or metabolic health. Just: height → BMI range → weight range. For a 5'10" man: 58 to 79 kg (128 to 174 lbs). A 46-pound range. Are all weights in that range equally healthy? Of course not. A 128-pound man with 8% body fat is very different from a 174-pound man with 30% body fat. Same height, same "healthy weight range," completely different health profiles. **The problem with ranges:** Ranges imply that anywhere inside is fine and anywhere outside is not. But health doesn't work that way. There's no cliff at BMI 25.0 where you suddenly become unhealthy. The risk curves are continuous. BMI 24.9 and 25.1 are essentially identical metabolically. But one is "healthy weight" and one is "overweight." The categories create artificial boundaries. And the lower end of the range is often too low. BMI 18.5 is the cutoff for underweight, but for many adults — especially older adults — BMI 19-20 is associated with higher mortality than BMI 23-25. The "U-shaped" mortality curve means the bottom of the "healthy" range isn't actually the healthiest point. **What I tell patients:**

I don't use "healthy weight" ranges in my practice. Instead, I use what I call the "metabolic sweet spot" — the weight range where your metabolic markers are optimal and you feel good. For some people, that's BMI 21. For others, it's BMI 26. There's no universal answer. I had a patient, 55-year-old woman, who spent years trying to get to 130 lbs (BMI 21) because a calculator told her that was her "healthy weight." She got there through extreme calorie restriction. Her periods stopped. Her bone density dropped. She was cold all the time. Her lipid panel actually worsened because her body was in starvation mode. We increased her calories, she went back to 145 lbs (BMI 23.5), and everything normalized. Was 130 "healthier"? The calculator said yes. Her body said no. **Age matters:** The "healthy weight" range doesn't account for age. But age changes everything. For adults over 65, BMI 25-27 is associated with lower mortality than BMI 18.5-22. The "obesity paradox" in elderly populations is well-documented. Extra weight provides reserve capacity during illness. Low BMI in older adults predicts frailty, falls, and mortality. So a 70-year-old trying to maintain the same BMI 22 they had at 30 may be doing themselves harm. Their "healthy weight" at 70 might be BMI 26. No calculator accounts for this. **The real question:** Instead of asking "what's my healthy weight?" ask "what weight allows me to: - Have good energy throughout the day - Sleep well - Maintain stable metabolic markers - Move comfortably - Feel good in my body" That weight might be higher or lower than the calculator's range. That's fine. Your body is more complex than a formula from the 1830s. Dr. David Chen P.S. Our calculator doesn't give "healthy weight ranges" for this exact reason. It's being lazy.
