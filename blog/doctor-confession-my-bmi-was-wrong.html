The Day I Realized My Own BMI Was Wrong — A Doctor's Confession

David Chen 2026-04-15 6 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
It was 2019. I was 42. I'd been practicing medicine for 12 years, lecturing patients about BMI, measuring waists, checking metabolic markers. I thought I had this figured out. Then I got my own physical. Height 5'10", weight 188 lbs. BMI 27.0. Overweight. I stared at the number for a solid minute. Me? The guy who ran 3 miles twice a week? Who ate reasonably well? Who'd never had an abnormal metabolic marker in his life? My first reaction was denial. The scale must be wrong. Or I was retaining water. Or... something. Anything but accepting that I, Dr. BMI-is-imperfect-but-useful, was technically overweight. Then I did what I tell patients to do. I checked the other markers. Waist: 34 inches. Ratio: 0.486. Under the 0.5 threshold. Fasting glucose: 89. BP: 118/76. LDL: 108. HDL: 52. All fine. I was BMI-overweight but metabolically healthy. The exact profile I describe to patients all the time. And I still felt weird about the number. **What it taught me:** Intellectually, I knew BMI was imperfect. I'd written about it, lectured about it, published on it. But emotionally? I still wanted the "normal" label. I still felt a sting seeing "overweight" on my chart. If a doctor who literally studies this stuff feels that way, what chance does the average patient have? It made me more empathetic. More careful with language. I stopped saying "your BMI is" and started saying "your BMI category is, but let's look at the full picture." The distinction matters. **What I did about it:** Nothing dramatic. I didn't go on a diet. I didn't join a gym. I made small adjustments: slightly smaller portions at dinner, one more run per week, less alcohol on weekends. Over 8 months, I dropped to 178 lbs. BMI 25.6. Still "overweight" by standard criteria. Still metabolically perfect.

Did I need to lose that weight? Clinically? Probably not. My markers were fine at 188. But I felt better at 178. More energy, better sleep, easier on my knees during runs. The weight loss was for quality of life, not medical necessity. **The broader lesson:** This experience crystallized something I'd been struggling to articulate. BMI is a category, not a verdict. "Overweight" doesn't mean "unhealthy." "Normal" doesn't mean "healthy." The categories are probabilistic, not deterministic. When I see a patient with BMI 27 and perfect metabolic markers, I don't push weight loss. I say: "Your BMI is elevated, but your body is functioning well. We can work on weight if you want, but medically, I'm not concerned." When I see BMI 22 and metabolic syndrome, I don't say "you're fine because your BMI is normal." I say: "Your BMI is misleading here. We need to address these metabolic markers urgently." **The honesty gap:** Most health content avoids this nuance because nuance doesn't go viral. "BMI is BS" gets clicks. "BMI is imperfect but still useful in context" doesn't. So the public gets polarized: either BMI is everything, or BMI is nothing. The truth is messier. BMI is a useful screening tool with known limitations. It works well for populations, less well for individuals. It misses some people who are unhealthy and flags some who aren't. It's one data point among many. And even knowing all that, I still felt a little thrill when my BMI dropped below 26. I'm human. The number has power, even when you know it's flawed. D. Chen P.S. Current weight: 181 lbs. BMI 26.0. Back in "overweight" after the holidays. Glucose still 90. Not worried. Will probably drop back to 178 by spring. Or not. Either way, my health is fine. The number is just a number.
