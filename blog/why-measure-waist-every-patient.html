Why I Measure Waist Circumference on Every Patient — Even the 'Normal' BMI Ones

Dr. Chen 2026-04-25 7 min read
Advertisement
Ad Space - 300x250
It takes 15 seconds. A tape measure around the abdomen, at the level of the iliac crest. Most patients are surprised — some are confused. "Doctor, I'm not here for weight loss," they'll say. "I'm here for my blood pressure." I measure anyway. Because waist circumference predicts cardiovascular risk better than BMI in almost every study. And because I've found too many surprises. **The data:** The INTERHEART study — 27,000 participants across 52 countries — found that waist-to-hip ratio predicted myocardial infarction better than BMI. The EPIC-Norfolk study showed that waist circumference was a better predictor of type 2 diabetes than BMI. A 2011 meta-analysis in The Lancet concluded that waist measures should replace BMI as the primary anthropometric measure for health risk. And yet, most physical exams skip it. Doctors are busy. Patients are self-conscious. The tape measure gets forgotten. **What I've found:** Patient A: BMI 23.2, "normal." Waist 37 inches. Fasting glucose 106. Triglycerides 165. Metabolic syndrome, hiding behind a normal BMI. Patient B: BMI 27.8, "overweight." Waist 33 inches. Glucose 88. HDL 62. Metabolically healthy, despite the BMI label. Patient C: BMI 24.5, right in the middle of "normal." Waist 39 inches. A1C 6.2. Pre-diabetic. His doctor had been telling him he was fine for three years because the BMI was green. These aren't rare cases. I see them weekly. BMI misses visceral fat. Waist circumference catches it. **The thresholds:** For metabolic risk, waist circumference thresholds are: - Men: >40 inches (102 cm) = elevated risk - Women: >35 inches (88 cm) = elevated risk - Asian populations: >35 inches (90 cm) for men, >31.5 inches (80 cm) for women

But I prefer waist-to-height ratio: waist divided by height. Threshold is 0.5. If your waist is more than half your height, your metabolic risk is elevated regardless of BMI. It's ethnicity-independent, sex-independent, and simple to explain. A 5'9" person (69 inches) with a 36-inch waist? Ratio = 0.52. Elevated risk, even though 36 inches is below the 40-inch threshold for men. The ratio catches what absolute circumference misses. **How to measure correctly:** Most people measure wrong. They measure at the belly button, or at the narrowest point. The correct location is midway between the lowest rib and the top of the iliac crest (hip bone), usually just above the belly button. Measure after exhaling normally. Don't suck in — that's cheating, and you're only cheating yourself. I keep a tape measure in every exam room. I offer to let patients measure themselves if they're uncomfortable. Some do, some let me do it. Either way, we get the number. **What to do with the number:** If waist is elevated but BMI is normal, we investigate. Glucose, lipids, blood pressure, liver enzymes. Often we find metabolic abnormalities that BMI missed. Sometimes everything is fine — some people just carry weight centrally without metabolic consequences. But we check. If waist and BMI are both elevated, we have a more serious conversation. Not about shame — about risk. Visceral fat is metabolically active. It releases inflammatory cytokines, impairs insulin signaling, promotes atherosclerosis. It's not cosmetic. It's biological. **The bottom line:** BMI is a screening tool. Waist circumference is a better screening tool. Together, they're more informative than either alone. Every physical exam should include both. If your doctor isn't measuring your waist, ask them to. Or measure it yourself and bring the number to your appointment. It takes 15 seconds. It could save your life. — Chen P.S. I measured my own waist this morning. 34 inches. Height 5'10". Ratio 0.49. Just under the threshold. Not bad for a guy who sits too much and eats too much hospital cafeteria food. Could be better. Will work on it.
